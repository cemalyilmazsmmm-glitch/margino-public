There's no price more public than a plain slice. It's on the window in foot-tall numbers. Everybody remembers what it *used* to be. Raise it a quarter and you'll hear about it from a guy who's been coming in since before you owned the place.

Which is exactly why so many pizza shops hold the line on the slice long after the math has turned against them. And in pizza, the math turns on **one ingredient that refuses to sit still.**

## The pie is mostly cheese — and cheese is a moving target

Strip a plain pie down to its costs and it's humbling how lopsided it is. Dough is cheap: flour, water, yeast, a little oil. Sauce is cheap: a can of good tomatoes goes a long way. The expensive part — by far — is the **mozzarella.** A pie can carry the better part of a pound of it.

And block mozzarella is famous for moving. It doesn't drift politely like most groceries; it **swings.** A shop can be buying cheese at one price in the spring and paying meaningfully more by mid-summer, for the exact same block, from the exact same supplier.

> When 60–70% of your food cost is sitting in one volatile ingredient, your pie cost isn't a number you set once. It's a number that changes while you're not looking.

## What a cheese swing does to a slice

Say a pie uses about a pound of mozz, and the block price climbs from **$2.50 to $4.00 a pound.** That's **+$1.50 on every pie** — and a pie is eight slices, so it's about **+19¢ a slice** in cheese alone, before you've touched the dough, the box, the gas, or the kid working the oven.

Hold the slice at **$3.00** through that swing and you haven't just lost a little. On a shop that moves hundreds of slices a day, **19 cents a slice is real money walking out the door** — and the whole-pie and delivery orders compound it.

## "But the slice is sacred"

It is. And you don't have to touch it blindly — you have to touch it *knowingly.* The shops that survive cheese swings tend to do some mix of:

- **Watch the cheese line specifically.** Not the whole invoice — the mozzarella unit cost, every delivery. That one number predicts your month.
- **Let the slice lag, but make the pie and toppings carry it.** A plain slice can hold for loyalty while the whole-pie, the Sicilian, and the specialty pies — where customers are less price-anchored — absorb the swing.
- **Price the apps for the cut.** A $3 slice and a $21 pie on DoorDash aren't $3 and $21 to you after a ~30% commission and the box. Delivery prices have to bake that in or every app order quietly underperforms the counter.

The point isn't to chase every nickel. It's to **know which number moved**, so the decision is deliberate instead of a vague sense that "it's tight this month."
